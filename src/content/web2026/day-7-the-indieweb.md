## Day 7: The IndieWeb

What, this class isn't about flexbox or CSS grid today? Have you been scammed? Not really.

After a week of code, we step back and remember why we're here in the first place: having our own creative spaces. This class was introduced as focusing on the small web, and today we expand that through the principles and ideas of the **IndieWeb**. From now till the end of class, our energies concentrate on our own websites. Today is (mostly) a no-code day, and quietly one of my favorite days of the whole course.

**Warm-up (10 min):** _One corner of the internet you'd save from a fire._ If the whole web were burning and you could rescue a single page, site, or community, what do you carry out? Ninety seconds each. Notice how few of us pick anything corporate.

**We'll explore:**

- Websites need not be what we traditionally think of them as. There can be conventional spaces on our websites, but also highly personal and individual patches of personality that you can't box into one thing.
- **The digital garden**: a website as a living, growing space you *tend*, not a stream you perform in, not a CV with a hero image. Gardens have half-finished beds, and that's their charm.
- Owning your data, writing for one person instead of an audience, and why "messy but personal beats polished but rented."

**The afternoon has two writing tasks** (by hand or in a plain text file; no code, no LLMs, this is your voice):

1. **Your manifesto and your plots.** What is your space for? Brainstorm the *plots* in your garden: an art journal, a gratitude list, a collection of things you're learning, playlists, projects. Then write a short personal manifesto for what this space is to be. We'll share these in dev notes, and I am genuinely looking forward to reading them.

2. **Your reply letter.** 💌 You've spent a week typesetting a letter from a great artist or thinker. Now you write back, or rather write *forward*: **a letter to a stranger who finds your website**. Who do you hope wanders in? What do you want them to know, feel, take with them? This becomes the second page of your Letters site this week, and if you look closely, it's also the seed of your personal site's about page and manifesto. You're not starting your project from a blank page; you're starting it from a letter you've already written.
